Module 1 · 1.1

Consent & Confidentiality

What is valid consent?

Before any dental treatment — including fluoride varnish application — you must obtain valid consent. Consent is only valid when it is:

  • Voluntary — the patient must not be pressured or coerced in any way
  • Informed — the patient must understand what the procedure involves, why it is recommended, what the alternatives are, and what happens if they decline
  • Given by a person with capacity — they must be able to understand, retain and weigh up the information and communicate a decision
Legal basis

The Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board [2015] UKSC 11 ruling confirmed that patients must be informed of all material risks — those a reasonable patient would want to know about. This applies to extended duties procedures.

Module 1 · 1.2

Consent: Children & Capacity

Children under 16

Consent is normally given by a parent or guardian. However, a child may consent independently if assessed as Gillick competent — meaning they can demonstrate sufficient understanding and maturity for the specific treatment (established in Gillick v West Norfolk AHA [1985]).

In practice, always seek both parental consent and the child's assent wherever possible.

Adults who lack capacity

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 governs decisions made on behalf of adults who lack capacity. Its five key principles:

  1. A person must be assumed to have capacity unless established otherwise
  2. All practicable steps must be taken to support decision-making before concluding someone lacks capacity
  3. Making an unwise decision does not mean a person lacks capacity
  4. Any act or decision must be done in the person's best interests
  5. The least restrictive option must always be considered
In practice

Always re-obtain consent at each visit — consent from a previous appointment does not carry forward. Check for changes in health, medication or allergies before every application.

Module 1 · 1.3

GDPR & Patient Confidentiality

The UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018 govern how patient information is handled. Patient data must be:

  • Collected only for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes
  • Limited to what is necessary — minimum necessary data only
  • Accurate and kept up to date
  • Stored securely and protected from unauthorised access
  • Retained only as long as necessary

Retention periods

  • Adults: minimum 11 years from the last entry
  • Children: until age 25 or 11 years from last entry, whichever is longer
Important

Discussing patient details outside the clinical team — including on social media — is a serious breach of confidentiality and may constitute a GDC fitness to practise concern.

Module 1 · 1.4

GDC Registrant Responsibilities

Standards for the Dental Team (2013) — Nine Principles

  1. Put patients' interests first
  2. Communicate effectively with patients
  3. Obtain valid consent
  4. Maintain and protect patients' information
  5. Have a clear and effective complaints procedure
  6. Work with colleagues in patients' best interests
  7. Maintain, develop and work within your professional knowledge and skills
  8. Raise concerns if patients are at risk
  9. Ensure personal behaviour maintains confidence in you and the profession

Scope of practice & 2026 reforms

GDC Scope of Practice (November 2025) confirms dental nurses may apply fluoride varnish if trained, competent and indemnified. The NHS Amendment Regulations 2026 (in force 1 April 2026) formally recognise standalone fluoride varnish by a dental nurse as an NHS course of treatment at 0.5 UDAs.

Module 1 · 1.5

Record Keeping

Good record keeping is both a professional and legal requirement under the Health and Social Care Act 2008. Your records must be:

  • Contemporaneous — written at the time of treatment, not retrospectively
  • Accurate and factual — record only what you observed and did
  • Clear and legible
  • Retained for the required statutory period

For each fluoride varnish application, record:

  • Date of treatment
  • Product name and concentration (ppm)
  • Surfaces treated
  • Confirmation that consent was obtained
  • Pre-treatment assessment findings
  • Aftercare advice given
  • Supervisor details (during training period)
Module 1 · 1.6

BLS & Anaphylaxis

Basic life support sequence (adult)

  1. Check for danger, then check for response
  2. Shout for help — call 999 and send for the AED
  3. Open airway (head tilt, chin lift), check for normal breathing ≤10 seconds
  4. 30 chest compressions (100–120/min, 5–6 cm depth, full recoil)
  5. 2 rescue breaths — continue 30:2 until help arrives

Anaphylaxis management

Emergency — act immediately

Recognise → Call 999 → Adrenaline 0.5mg IM (1:1000) outer thigh without delay → Lie flat, legs raised → BLS ready → Repeat adrenaline after 5 min if no improvement.

Colophony — a resin constituent of many fluoride varnishes — is a recognised allergen. Always check for allergy before every application.

Module 1 · 1.7

Fluoride Toxicity

The estimated toxic dose is 5mg per kg of body weight. A standard application (0.25–0.5ml of 22,600ppm varnish) contains approximately 5–11mg — well below the toxic threshold for an adult, but relevant for very small children.

Signs of acute toxicity

  • Nausea, vomiting and abdominal pain
  • Excessive salivation and headache
  • Dizziness — in severe cases: convulsions, hypotension, cardiac arrhythmia

Management

  1. Do not induce vomiting
  2. Give milk or water if patient is conscious and able to swallow
  3. Call 999 and alert the rest of the dental team
  4. Monitor vital signs and perform BLS if required
  5. Document the incident fully

Module 2 · 2.1

Health & Safety — Legal Framework

  • Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 — duty on all employees to take reasonable care of their own and others' safety
  • Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 — employers must carry out suitable risk assessments
  • COSHH 2002 — fluoride varnish is a chemical substance; your practice must hold a COSHH assessment for fluoride products

COSHH in practice

  • Read the material safety data sheet (MSDS/SDS) for each product
  • Store fluoride varnish as per manufacturer instructions — typically cool, dry, away from patients
  • Never use a product after its expiry date
  • Dispose of unused varnish and single-use applicators as clinical waste
Module 2 · 2.2

Cross Infection Control

Standard precautions

Standard precautions must be applied to every patient, every time, regardless of their known infection status. Treat all blood and bodily fluids as potentially infectious.

The legal and professional framework for IPC in dentistry is set out in Health Technical Memorandum 01-05 (HTM 01-05), which applies to all registered dental settings in England. Equivalent guidance: SDCEP (Scotland), Wales and Northern Ireland.

Key principle

You cannot tell whether a patient carries a bloodborne virus from their appearance, history or behaviour. Standard precautions protect both patients and you from cross-contamination at every appointment.

Module 2 · 2.3

PPE & Hand Hygiene

PPE for fluoride varnish application

  • Operator: non-sterile examination gloves, fluid-resistant surgical mask (FRSM), protective eyewear
  • Patient: bib and protective eyewear

Don PPE before patient contact. Remove and dispose of correctly after each patient. Change gloves if they tear during the procedure.

Hand hygiene — WHO 6-step technique

  1. Palm to palm
  2. Back of each hand
  3. Palm to palm, fingers interlaced
  4. Backs of fingers interlocked
  5. Each thumb in a rotating grip
  6. Circular motion of fingertips on palm

Perform hand hygiene before donning and after removing gloves, and between patients.

Module 2 · 2.4

Decontamination & Sterilisation

The three-stage cycle

  1. Cleaning — removal of visible contamination by manual or ultrasonic cleaning, followed by rinsing
  2. Disinfection — reduction of microbial load to a safe level
  3. Sterilisation — validated autoclave per HTM 01-05. Class B autoclaves required for wrapped instruments; Class N for unwrapped only

Between each patient

  • Application brushes and pledgets are single-use only — never reuse
  • Decontaminate all clinical contact surfaces with a compatible surface disinfectant
  • Remove and replace protective barriers
  • Dispose of all single-use items as clinical waste
Module 2 · 2.5

How Fluoride Prevents Caries

Fluoride exerts its caries-preventive effects through three mechanisms:

  • Remineralisation — promotes deposition of fluorapatite into demineralised enamel, producing a surface more resistant to acid attack than the original hydroxyapatite
  • Inhibition of demineralisation — fluorapatite is less soluble in the weak organic acids produced by cariogenic bacteria
  • Antibacterial action — inhibits enzyme systems of Streptococcus mutans, reducing acid production from fermentable sugars
Evidence

Systematic reviews have demonstrated caries reduction rates of up to 37% in the primary dentition and 43% in the permanent dentition with regular fluoride varnish application.

Module 2 · 2.6

Products, Evidence & Storage

Delivering Better Oral Health (DBOH)

The primary UK evidence base for topical fluoride. Current recommendations:

  • Children aged 3+ and adults at moderate/high caries risk: 22,600ppm fluoride varnish, at least twice a year
  • Children under 3 with particular caries risk: at the clinical judgement of the prescribing dentist

Products

  • Most common: 22,600ppm (5%) sodium fluoride — Duraphat, Colmar and others
  • Standard dose: 0.25–0.5ml per patient
  • Follow the prescribing dentist's instructions and manufacturer's guidance

Storage

  • Cool, dry place — protected from direct sunlight
  • Check expiry date before each use; never use expired product
  • Store out of reach of patients — some formulations are prescription-only medicines
Module 2 · 2.7

Patient Groups

Pre-school (under 5)

Parental consent essential. Tell-show-do technique. Short, calm, positive appointments. Knee-to-knee positioning if needed.

Children 5–12

Parental consent + child's assent. Simple positive language. Praise throughout. Distraction for anxious children.

Teenagers

May be Gillick competent. Respect autonomy. Avoid patronising language. Oral hygiene motivation especially important.

Adults

Full medical/drug history. Check contraindications. Clear communication. Allow time for questions.

Elderly patients

Complex medical histories and polypharmacy. Xerostomia common — fluoride especially beneficial. Target exposed root surfaces. May need adapted positioning, communication and longer appointments.

Module 3 · 3.1

Anatomy of the Oral Cavity

Before applying fluoride varnish, carry out a brief intra-oral assessment. You must recognise normal and abnormal appearances of oral tissues.

Structures to assess

  • Lips and labial mucosa — ulceration, vesicles, swelling, lesions
  • Buccal mucosa — white/red patches, ulceration, linea alba, cheek biting
  • Hard and soft palate — lesions, swelling, palatine tori
  • Tongue — coating, ulceration, geographic tongue, fissuring
  • Floor of mouth — swelling, cysts, unusual vascularity
  • Gingivae — inflammation, recession, bleeding
  • Teeth — caries, fractures, erosion, staining, hypoplasia
Module 3 · 3.2

When to Defer & Refer

Do not apply fluoride varnish if you observe any of the following. Defer treatment and refer to the supervising dentist:

  • Active ulceration or inflammatory lesions in the area to be treated
  • Visible cavitation or caries requiring operative assessment
  • Active gingival bleeding
  • Unexplained swelling, lumps or soft tissue abnormality
  • Patient declines consent
Scope of practice reminder

You are not expected to diagnose pathology. Your role is to recognise that something needs further assessment and refer appropriately. When in doubt — defer and refer.

Module 3 · 3.3

Treatments Requiring Fluoride & Prescription

Fluoride varnish is recommended as part of a preventive plan for patients with:

  • Moderate or high caries risk per DBOH
  • Early/active carious lesions managed by prevention-first approach
  • Fixed orthodontic treatment (increased risk around brackets)
  • Xerostomia (medication, radiotherapy, Sjögren's syndrome)
  • Exposed root surfaces and root caries risk
  • Involvement in community oral health programmes
Important

Fluoride varnish is always applied on the prescription of a GDC-registered dentist. You do not make the clinical decision — you carry out the application competently, safely and in line with the prescription.

Module 3 · 3.4

Equipment Required

  • Fluoride varnish — correct product, in date, per the prescription
  • Application brush or pledget — single-use
  • Dental mirror and cheek retractors or lip retractor
  • Cotton wool rolls and dry guards
  • Saliva ejector and aspirator
  • Three-in-one syringe (for drying)
  • Operator PPE: gloves, fluid-resistant surgical mask, eye protection
  • Patient PPE: bib and protective eyewear
Checklist tip

Set up and check all equipment before seating the patient. Confirm the product expiry date and verify the prescription is in place before beginning the appointment.

Module 3 · 3.5

Application Technique

  1. Confirm prescription is in place. Review patient records and medical history.
  2. Wash hands and don full PPE. Seat patient, apply bib and protective eyewear.
  3. Carry out intra-oral assessment. Document findings.
  4. Obtain (or re-confirm) consent. Document.
  5. Isolate: retract cheek, place cotton wool rolls, position saliva ejector.
  6. Dry tooth surfaces with three-in-one syringe or cotton wool.
  7. Load application brush — total dose 0.25–0.5ml per patient.
  8. Apply varnish systematically from posterior to anterior. Prioritise: occlusal pits and fissures, approximal margins, exposed root surfaces.
  9. Treat each quadrant in turn; replace cotton wool rolls as needed.
  10. Remove isolation materials. Allow patient to close naturally — varnish sets on contact with saliva.
  11. Provide aftercare advice — verbal and written where possible.
  12. Complete full contemporaneous clinical records.
Module 3 · 3.6

Aftercare Advice

Instruct the patient (and parent/carer where applicable) to:

  • No rinsing, drinking or eating for 30 minutes after application
  • Avoid hard or sticky foods for 4 hours
  • Do not brush teeth until the following morning
  • A thin coating on teeth is normal and will gradually wear off over 24–48 hours
Take the opportunity

Reinforce oral hygiene advice and dietary guidance at every appointment as part of a holistic preventive approach. Fluoride varnish works best alongside twice-daily brushing with fluoride toothpaste, reduced sugar intake and regular recalls.

Module 3 · 3.7

Communication

✓ Good patient management

  • Welcome the patient by name and introduce yourself
  • Explain each step before you carry it out
  • Check understanding and invite questions
  • Adapt your communication to the patient's age and ability
  • Respond calmly if the patient becomes anxious
  • Give clear and consistent aftercare advice

✗ Poor patient management

  • Beginning treatment without explanation
  • Using clinical jargon the patient cannot understand
  • Dismissing or ignoring the patient's concerns
  • Failing to adapt for anxious or vulnerable patients
  • Providing inconsistent aftercare advice
  • Proceeding without valid consent

Communication methods

  • Verbal — clear, jargon-free, adapted to patient's age and understanding
  • Written — information leaflets and printed aftercare instructions
  • Visual aids — models and diagrams for children or patients with learning disabilities
  • Interpreter services — professional interpreter where a language barrier exists; avoid relying on family members
Module 3 · 3.8

Managing Patients Who Decline Fluoride

Common reasons for refusal

  • Online misinformation about fluoride toxicity or systemic harm
  • Opposition to water fluoridation extending to all fluoride products
  • Concerns about fluorosis risk in children
  • Previous negative experience, cultural values or personal preference

How to respond

  1. Listen without judgment — allow the patient to explain their concern fully
  2. Acknowledge their concern — do not dismiss or immediately counter it
  3. Provide accurate information — fluoride is naturally occurring; varnish has been used safely in the UK since the 1980s; the dose is very small; there is no credible evidence of systemic harm from correctly applied topical fluoride
  4. Respect autonomy — if fully informed and still declining, that is their right; you must not proceed without consent
  5. Document the discussion — record that refusal was informed and that information was provided
  6. Inform the supervising dentist
Key principle

Your role is to inform, not to debate. A patient who feels genuinely listened to is far more likely to reconsider at a future appointment than one who feels lectured.
