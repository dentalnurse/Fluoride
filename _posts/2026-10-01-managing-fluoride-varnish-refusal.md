Fluoride varnish refusal happens. A parent declines on behalf of 
their child, an adult patient says they have heard fluoride is 
harmful, or someone simply does not want it that day. How you 
handle that moment matters both clinically and professionally.

This post covers what the GDC expects when a patient refuses 
treatment, how to have that conversation well, and why your 
documentation is so important. For a full guide to the GDC 
requirements around applying fluoride varnish independently, 
read our post on [GDC scope of practice for dental nurses](https://dentalnurse.github.io/Fluoride/blog/can-dental-nurses-apply-fluoride-varnish-gdc-scope-of-practice-2025/).

## What the GDC Says About Refusal

The GDC's Standards for the Dental Team are clear. Patients have 
the right to withdraw their consent at any time, to refuse 
treatment, or to ask for it to be stopped after it has started. 
Dental professionals must ensure their patients are aware of 
these rights and must observe their patients' wishes.

Standard 3.3 requires that a patient's consent remains valid at 
each stage of treatment. By valid, the GDC means the patient 
must have freely given their consent, having been given the 
opportunity to discuss and consider the range of options 
available to them, including having no treatment at all.

This means refusal is not a failure on your part. It is a 
patient exercising a right that the GDC explicitly protects. 
Your job is to make sure they have the information they need 
to make that decision, to respect it, and to record it 
accurately.

## Before the Conversation: Why Information Matters

The GDC is clear that dental professionals are required to find 
out what their patients want to know, as well as what they think 
their patients need to know. This applies to fluoride varnish 
just as it does to any other treatment.

Before a patient refuses, the best thing you can do is make sure 
they have good information in the first place. This means 
explaining at the start of the appointment:

- What fluoride varnish is and why it is being recommended for 
  them or their child
- What the evidence says about its effectiveness
- What the application involves and how long it takes
- The aftercare involved
- That they can ask questions or decline

A patient who understands what you are recommending and why is 
less likely to refuse out of uncertainty, and more likely to make 
a genuinely informed decision either way.

## Common Reasons for Refusal and How to Respond

**"I have heard fluoride is harmful"**

This is one of the most common reasons, often driven by 
misinformation online. You can explain calmly that fluoride 
varnish has been used safely for decades and is recommended by 
the UK Chief Dental Officers in Delivering Better Oral Health, 
the evidence-based guidance used across the UK. For a plain-English 
guide to what DBOH says, read our post on [what Delivering Better 
Oral Health means for dental nurses](https://dentalnurse.github.io/Fluoride/blog/what-is-delivering-better-oral-health/). 
Do not argue or dismiss the concern. Acknowledge it, give 
accurate information, and leave the decision with the patient.

**"My child does not like it"**

For children who are anxious or have had a difficult experience 
before, this is a real and valid concern. You can talk through 
what the application involves, use a tell-show-do approach, 
and offer to apply to just a few teeth first if the child is 
willing. If the child or parent still declines, respect that.

**"We do not do fluoride for religious or personal reasons"**

This requires no debate from you. Acknowledge the decision 
respectfully, record it, and move on. It is not your role to 
challenge personal or religious beliefs.

**"Not today, maybe next time"**

Sometimes refusal is situational rather than absolute. A child 
who is unwell, a parent who is in a rush, or a patient who 
simply does not want it at that particular appointment. You can 
note this, mention that it can be offered at the next visit, 
and leave the door open without any pressure.

## What Not to Do

The GDC is clear that patients must not be pressured or pushed 
into treatment they do not want. Concerns have been raised with 
the GDC about dental professionals trying to persuade patients 
to undergo treatment against their wishes. This can lead to 
fitness to practise proceedings.

In practice this means:

- Do not repeatedly push after an initial refusal
- Do not make the patient or parent feel guilty
- Do not imply their child will come to harm as a result of 
  declining
- Do not record the refusal in a way that implies the patient 
  made a wrong decision

## Documentation: What You Need to Record

This is arguably the most important part of managing refusal 
professionally. Your clinical record is your evidence that you 
acted appropriately.

When a patient or parent declines fluoride varnish, record:

- The date
- That fluoride varnish was offered and declined
- A brief note of the reason given, where the patient or parent 
  has shared one
- That information was provided about the benefits of the 
  treatment
- Whether the refusal was for this appointment only or stated 
  as an ongoing preference

You do not need to write an essay. A clear, contemporaneous 
note is sufficient. What matters is that anyone reading the 
record later can see that the treatment was offered, the 
patient was informed, and their decision was respected.

The GDC has been clear that failure to keep adequate patient 
records is one of the most common issues in fitness to practise 
cases. Good documentation protects both the patient and you.

## Refusal in the Context of the NHS Sub-band 1 Treatment

If you are delivering fluoride varnish under the NHS sub-band 1 
course of treatment introduced in April 2026, refusal has an 
additional administrative dimension. If a patient declines 
treatment at an appointment, you will need to follow your 
practice's process for managing the course of treatment in 
the NHS BSA system accordingly. For a full guide to the NHS 
changes, read our post on [NHS fluoride varnish changes 2026](https://dentalnurse.github.io/Fluoride/blog/nhs-fluoride-varnish-changes-2026/).

This is a practice-level process and your practice lead or 
prescribing clinician should be able to advise on the correct 
steps.

## The Bigger Picture

Managing refusal well is part of what it means to be a 
competent, patient-centred clinician. The GDC's approach to 
consent is built on shared decision-making, the idea that 
patients and dental professionals each bring expertise to a 
treatment conversation, and that the patient's wishes must 
be respected.

A patient who declines fluoride varnish but feels heard and 
respected is far more likely to accept it next time. One who 
feels pressured or dismissed may not come back at all.
