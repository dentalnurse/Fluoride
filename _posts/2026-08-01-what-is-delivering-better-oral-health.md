If you have done any dental nursing training, sat any exams, or 
completed any post-registration qualifications, you will have come 
across the phrase Delivering Better Oral Health. It gets referenced 
constantly. But not everyone has actually sat down and read it, and 
even fewer could explain what it really means for their day-to-day 
practice.

This post is a plain-English overview of what DBOH is, what it covers, 
and why it matters specifically for dental nurses.

## What Is Delivering Better Oral Health?

Delivering Better Oral Health, usually abbreviated to DBOH, is an 
evidence-based toolkit for prevention published jointly by the 
Department of Health and Social Care, NHS England, the Welsh 
Government and the Department of Health Northern Ireland. It has 
been developed with the support of all four UK Chief Dental Officers.

It was first published in 2007 and has been updated several times 
since. The current version, the fourth edition, was last updated in 
September 2025.

The primary audience is dental teams providing frontline care. It 
is designed to support dental professionals in routinely promoting 
oral health and preventing oral disease, drawing on a UK-wide 
collaboration of well-respected experts including frontline dental 
teams and patient representatives.

## What Does It Cover?

DBOH covers four main oral diseases:

- Dental caries
- Periodontal disease
- Oral cancer
- Tooth wear

For each of these it provides evidence-based recommendations on 
prevention, organised into summary tables in Chapter 2. These tables 
set out what advice and clinical interventions should be offered to 
all patients, and what additional support should be provided for 
those at higher risk.

Beyond the disease-specific chapters, DBOH also covers:

- The use of fluoride
- Oral hygiene and toothbrushing
- Diet and sugar reduction
- Tobacco cessation
- Alcohol
- Behaviour change

## How Are the Recommendations Structured?

This is worth understanding properly because it affects how you 
interpret and apply the guidance.

DBOH uses the GRADE system to classify its recommendations. Each 
recommendation is rated based on the quality of the evidence 
behind it:

**Strong recommendations** mean the evidence clearly supports the 
intervention. The guidance development group is highly confident 
that the benefits outweigh any risks.

**Conditional recommendations** mean the evidence is less certain, 
or the balance between benefits and risks is less clear. A 
conditional recommendation does not mean the intervention does 
not work. It means the current evidence is not of the highest 
certainty.

**Good practice points** are based on clinical consensus and expert 
opinion rather than research directly rated for certainty. They 
reflect established clinical practice.

Understanding this matters because it helps you explain to patients 
why you are recommending something, and it helps you understand the 
difference between what is firmly evidence-based and what is based 
on clinical consensus.

## Who Does the Guidance Apply To?

DBOH is not just for dentists. It is for the whole dental team. The 
guidance specifically states it supports primary care dental teams 
to routinely promote oral and general health, and it is an 
educational resource for dental schools and postgraduate training 
providers.

For dental nurses this means DBOH is relevant to your practice 
every time you give oral health advice, apply fluoride varnish, 
discuss diet with a patient, or support a patient with behaviour 
change around their oral hygiene.

## The Risk-Based Approach

One of the most important principles running through DBOH is that 
care and advice should be tailored to each patient's individual 
risk. The guidance is clear that each patient's risk needs to be 
assessed at every dental recall visit and monitored across the 
life course, because disease risk will change over time.

Patients considered to be giving concern, meaning those at higher 
risk of dental disease or for whom disease would present a 
significant challenge, include:

- Children or adults presenting with current or past dental disease
- People with physical or learning disabilities
- Medically compromised patients, for example those with diabetes
- Mentally and physically frail older people including those with 
  cognitive decline
- People undergoing treatments that increase disease risk, for 
  example chemotherapy or drug therapy causing dry mouth
- People experiencing social disadvantage or homelessness

This risk-based framework is directly relevant to fluoride varnish 
application. When you assess a patient's caries risk before 
treatment, review their care plan, and decide on the frequency 
of application, you are applying the DBOH risk-based approach 
in practice. For a full guide to the clinical steps involved, 
read our post on [how to apply fluoride varnish](https://dentalnurse.github.io/Fluoride/blog/how-to-apply-fluoride-varnish-step-by-step-guide-dental-nurses/).

## Why Dental Nurses Should Know It

There are a few practical reasons why DBOH matters beyond just 
passing exams.

**It underpins your clinical decision-making.** When you apply 
fluoride varnish, assess a patient's diet, or give oral hygiene 
advice, DBOH is the evidence base behind those recommendations. 
Knowing where the guidance comes from makes you a more confident 
and more credible clinician.

**It supports your conversations with patients.** Being able to 
explain why you are recommending something, and how the advice 
is based on research rather than personal opinion, helps patients 
understand and act on what you are telling them.

**It is referenced throughout post-registration training.** Whether 
you are completing a fluoride varnish qualification, a radiography 
course, or an oral health education programme, DBOH will come up. 
Understanding it properly rather than just knowing its name makes 
a real difference to the quality of your written work and your 
practical assessments.

**It is a living document.** DBOH is updated regularly as new 
evidence emerges. Keeping up with changes is part of your 
professional development as a dental nurse and contributes to your 
GDC CPD requirements. For a full guide to what CPD requires, read 
our post on [CPD requirements for dental nurses](https://dentalnurse.github.io/Fluoride/blog/cpd-requirements-for-dental-nurses/).

DBOH is freely available on GOV.UK. You can read the full guidance 
including all chapters and summary tables at:

[Delivering Better Oral Health: an evidence-based toolkit for 
prevention](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/delivering-better-oral-health-an-evidence-based-toolkit-for-prevention)

useful starting point if you want to understand how the 
recommendations apply to specific patient groups.
