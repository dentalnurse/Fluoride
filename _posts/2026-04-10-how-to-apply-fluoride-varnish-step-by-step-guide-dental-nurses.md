Fluoride varnish is one of the most evidence-based preventive tools 
available in dentistry. For dental nurses with appropriate training in 
fluoride varnish application, applying it correctly is both a clinical 
skill and a professional responsibility. This guide walks through every 
stage of the procedure, from arriving at the surgery to writing up 
the record.

## Before You Start: What the GDC Requires

Under the GDC's Scope of Practice (November 2025), dental nurses may 
apply fluoride varnish provided they are trained, competent and 
appropriately indemnified or insured for the task. The GDC does not 
specify a particular qualification - what matters is that your training 
has resulted in assessed competence, and that there is mutual agreement 
between you and your dentist, employer or supervisor that you are ready 
to practise independently.

Before you carry out the procedure you need:

- Appropriate training in fluoride varnish application resulting in 
  assessed competence
- A prescription or patient-specific direction (PSD) from a 
  GDC-registered dentist
- Indemnity cover that specifically includes extended duties
  
If you are unsure whether your indemnity covers you, contact your 
provider before carrying out the procedure.

## Step 1: Patient Assessment

Always assess the patient before applying fluoride varnish. Check for:

- **Contraindications** -- active ulceration or stomatitis in the area 
  to be treated; known allergy to colophony (a rosin found in many 
  varnishes); severe or poorly controlled asthma (some varnishes contain 
  alcohol -- check the product SDS). If any contraindication is present, 
  defer treatment and document your reasoning.
- **Caries risk** -- review the patient's Delivering Better Oral Health 
  (DBOH) risk status. DBOH recommends fluoride varnish at least twice a 
  year for children and adults where there is clinical concern or elevated 
  caries risk.
- **Soft tissue** -- a brief visual inspection of the teeth and mucosa 
  you intend to treat. Note any abnormalities and refer to the dentist 
  if anything looks unusual.

## Step 2: Consent

Obtain informed, verbal consent at every appointment - even if the 
patient has had the procedure before. Explain:

- What you are doing and why
- The aftercare instructions (no eating or drinking for 30 minutes; 
  avoid brushing until the following morning to maximise fluoride 
  contact time)
- That the varnish may leave a temporary yellow tint that fades 
  within 24 hours

For children, obtain consent from a parent or legal guardian unless 
the child is Gillick competent. For very young children, use the 
knee-to-knee technique with a parent - sit facing the parent with 
knees touching, lay the child across both laps with the head resting 
on your knees.

## Step 3: Preparation and PPE

Standard PPE for fluoride varnish application:

- Non-sterile examination gloves
- Fluid-resistant surgical mask
- Eye protection (your own and the patient's)

Prepare your tray with:

- Single-dose fluoride varnish (standard UK concentration: 22,600 ppm, 
  equivalent to 5% sodium fluoride)
- Single-use application brush or microbrush
- Cotton wool rolls or dry guards for moisture control
- Dental mirror and good light source
- 3-in-1 syringe

## Step 4: Application

1. **Dry the teeth** -- use the 3-in-1 air syringe to gently dry the 
   teeth. Varnish sets on contact with saliva but adheres better to a 
   relatively dry surface. Do not over-dry as this is uncomfortable 
   for patients.
2. **Place moisture control** -- cotton wool rolls in the buccal sulci 
   help manage saliva flow. You do not need rubber dam.
3. **Apply the varnish** -- use the single-use brush to apply a thin 
   layer to the tooth surfaces. Work systematically, usually starting 
   posteriorly and working anteriorly. Prioritise:
   - Occlusal pits and fissures
   - Approximal margins (the contact areas between teeth)
   - Exposed root surfaces in patients with recession or xerostomia

   The recommended dose is 0.25-0.5 ml per appointment (roughly half 
   to a full single-dose capsule). Do not over-apply -- a thin coat 
   is sufficient.
4. **Allow to set** -- fluoride varnish sets on contact with saliva. 
   The patient can close their mouth as soon as you have finished.

## Step 5: Aftercare Instructions

Before the patient leaves, confirm:

- No eating or drinking for 30 minutes (if the patient is a young 
  child, factor this into appointment timing)
- Avoid hard or sticky foods for the rest of the day
- Do not brush teeth until the following morning -- this gives the 
  fluoride maximum contact time with the enamel
- Yellow tint is normal and will fade as the varnish wears off over 
  the next 24 hours

Provide aftercare in writing where possible, particularly for parents 
taking children home.

## Step 6: Waste Disposal and Decontamination

- The single-use application brush goes in clinical waste (yellow bag) 
  -- it has been in contact with mucous membranes
- The fluoride varnish packaging follows your practice's clinical 
  waste stream
- Clean and decontaminate the dental mirror according to HTM 01-05 
  (clean, disinfect, autoclave if reusable)
- Remove PPE and perform hand hygiene

## Step 7: Documentation

Record the following in the patient's notes at the time of treatment:

- Date of application
- Patient identifier and age group
- Product name and concentration used
- Clinical assessment findings (contraindications checked, soft tissue 
  normal)
- Consent obtained (note from whom -- patient or guardian)
- Tooth surfaces treated
- Post-operative instructions given
- Prescribing clinician name and GDC number

Thorough records protect you professionally. If a query arises later, 
your notes are your evidence that you practised safely and within your 
scope.

## How Often Should Fluoride Varnish Be Applied?

Delivering Better Oral Health (DBOH) recommends fluoride varnish at 
least twice a year for patients where there is clinical concern or 
elevated caries risk. This includes:

- Children and adults at moderate or high caries risk
- Older adults with xerostomia, root exposure or complex medical 
  histories

For children receiving fluoride varnish under the NHS sub-band 1 
course of treatment introduced in April 2026, the schedule is 
determined by the care plan and must be at least 3 months apart.

## Key Takeaway

Fluoride varnish application is a straightforward procedure when you 
follow a systematic approach. The clinical steps themselves are quick; 
most of the value comes from thorough assessment, clear communication 
with the patient, and a complete clinical record. Done well, it is one 
of the most impactful preventive interventions you can offer.
