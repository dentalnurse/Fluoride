Indemnity is one of those topics that comes up early in a dental 
nursing career and somehow stays confusing for years. A lot of dental 
nurses assume they are covered by their practice and leave it at that. 
Sometimes that is true. Sometimes it is not. And if something goes 
wrong, it is you who has to face the GDC, not your employer.

This post covers what the GDC actually requires, the bits that often 
catch people out, and what you specifically need to check if you are 
carrying out extended duties like fluoride varnish application. For a 
full explanation of the GDC requirements around extended duties, read 
our post on [GDC scope of practice for dental nurses](https://dentalnurse.github.io/Fluoride/blog/can-dental-nurses-apply-fluoride-varnish-gdc-scope-of-practice-2025/).

## Is Indemnity Actually a Legal Requirement?

Yes. It is a legal requirement for every GDC registrant to have 
appropriate indemnity or insurance in place before practising. This 
applies to dental nurses just as much as it does to dentists.

The GDC's Standards for the Dental Team, Standard 1.8, states that 
you must have appropriate arrangements in place for patients to seek 
compensation if they have suffered harm. Practising without indemnity 
is a serious matter. The GDC will usually investigate allegations that 
a dental professional is practising without adequate cover, and it 
could put your registration at risk.

You are also required to declare that you have appropriate indemnity 
in place every year as part of your GDC registration renewal.

## Can I Be Covered by My Employer?

Yes. Many dental practices hold an indemnity policy that covers their 
employed dental nurses, and this is perfectly acceptable to the GDC 
as long as the level of cover is sufficient for your role.

However, the GDC is very clear on one thing. Your employer may have 
a policy which provides you with cover, but it is your responsibility 
to make sure you are covered for all the tasks that you do, the 
locations where you work and the hours you work. Do not assume that 
you are covered. Ask your employer for a copy of the policy and keep 
it for your records.

Verbal reassurance is not enough. You need written confirmation.

It is also worth knowing that some employer policies cover clinical 
negligence but not GDC fitness to practise investigations. These are 
two separate things. If you want support in the event of a fitness to 
practise investigation, check whether your cover includes this or 
whether you need additional cover.

## What If I Am Carrying Out Extended Duties?

This is the bit that catches a lot of dental nurses out, particularly 
those taking on extended duties for the first time.

The GDC is clear that you must regularly review your cover to ensure 
it applies across all the tasks that you do. If your scope of practice 
changes, for example you start applying fluoride varnish independently, 
you need to make sure your indemnity covers that before you start.

If you are on employer cover, ask your practice to confirm in writing 
that your extended duties are included. If they are not covered, you 
need to either ask the practice to extend their policy or arrange your 
own individual cover.

This is not optional. The GDC states that you must ensure you have 
the correct cover in place before you make changes to your practice. 
Carrying out an extended duty without appropriate indemnity puts you 
in breach of GDC standards and leaves you personally exposed if a 
complaint or claim arises.

## What Types of Cover Are Available?

The GDC recognises three main types of cover:

**Personal cover** -- a policy in your own name, obtained from an 
indemnifier or insurance provider. Some professional associations 
offer cover as part of membership.

**Employer-based cover** -- your employer's policy covers you for 
work carried out within your agreed role. As above, you must confirm 
in writing what this does and does not include.

**NHS or Crown indemnity** -- if you are directly employed by the 
NHS, you may be covered through a clinical negligence scheme. 
However, this only covers work done under the terms of your NHS 
employment. It does not cover private practice, and it does not 
cover GDC fitness to practise proceedings.

The GDC advises you to consider your own situation carefully and 
seek advice from your employer, professional association or an 
indemnity adviser to make sure the level of cover is right for you.

## Questions to Ask Before You Start Practising

The GDC suggests asking yourself the following before arranging 
or reviewing your cover:

1. Will this cover me for all the tasks I do, the locations where 
   I work and the hours I work?
2. Does it cover me for any extended duties I carry out?
3. Is the level of financial cover sufficient for my individual 
   practice?
4. Does it cover GDC fitness to practise proceedings, or do I 
   need additional cover for this?
5. What happens if I make changes to my practice, such as taking 
   on new tasks or changing my hours?
6. If my policy is claims-made rather than occurrence-based, do 
   I have run-off cover for claims that arise after the policy 
   expires?

If you cannot answer these confidently, it is worth speaking to 
your employer or a professional association before your next 
clinical session.

## The Bigger Picture

Indemnity exists to protect patients first and foremost. If 
something goes wrong during treatment, patients have the right 
to seek compensation. Your indemnity is what makes that possible.

It also protects you. Without appropriate cover you could face 
GDC fitness to practise proceedings, and you would be personally 
liable for any compensation and legal costs.

It is one of the less exciting parts of being a dental nurse. 
But getting it right is one of the most important things you 
can do for your patients and your career.
