Before applying fluoride varnish, a thorough pre-treatment assessment is not just good practice - it is a GDC requirement. Identifying a contraindication and deferring treatment appropriately is a mark of clinical competence, not a failure. This article covers the contraindications you need to know, and what to do when you find one.

<!--more-->

## Why Contraindication Screening Matters

Fluoride varnish is a safe and well-evidenced preventive intervention. However, like any clinical procedure, it carries a small number of absolute contraindications and several factors that require clinical judgement before proceeding. Failing to screen adequately could result in harm to the patient, a complaint, or a fitness to practise referral.

Your clinical record must show that you checked for contraindications at every appointment - not just the first one.

---

## Absolute Contraindications

These are circumstances in which fluoride varnish **must not** be applied until the contraindication is resolved.

### 1. Active Ulceration or Stomatitis

Do not apply fluoride varnish to any area of the mouth where there is active ulceration, open wounds or stomatitis. The alcohol content of some varnishes, and the varnish itself, can cause pain and delay healing. This includes:

- Aphthous ulcers (mouth ulcers) in the area to be treated
- Traumatic ulcers from dentures or sharp teeth
- Mucositis in patients undergoing chemotherapy

**Important:** if the ulceration is localised (for example, a single ulcer on the lower lip), you may be able to apply varnish to unaffected areas while deferring treatment of the area immediately adjacent to the ulcer. Document your decision-making clearly.

### 2. Known Allergy to Colophony (Rosin)

Colophony is a resin found in many fluoride varnishes - it is what gives the varnish its sticky, adherent quality. Patients with a known colophony or rosin allergy, or a related allergy to sticking plasters (which also often contain rosin), may react to standard fluoride varnishes.

**What to do:** check the patient's medical history for any mention of rosin, colophony or plaster allergy. If an allergy is confirmed, check whether a colophony-free varnish is available in your practice. Some formulations (for example, certain Duraphat alternatives) are colophony-free. If in doubt, defer and seek advice from the prescribing clinician.

## Contraindications Requiring Careful Assessment

These are not absolute bars to treatment, but require clinical judgement - and in some cases, discussion with the prescribing clinician before proceeding.

### 3. Asthma

The relationship between fluoride varnish and asthma is product-dependent. Some varnishes (including standard Duraphat) contain **ethanol (alcohol)** as a carrier. In patients with asthma, inhaled alcohol vapour can theoretically trigger bronchospasm in sensitive individuals.

The risk with fluoride varnish is extremely low - the amount of alcohol vapour generated is tiny - but you should:

- Note asthma status during your pre-treatment assessment
- Check the product's Summary of Data Safety (SDS) for alcohol content
- Be aware of the patient's severity of asthma and current control
- Consider a non-alcohol-based alternative if the patient has severe, poorly controlled asthma

In practice, the vast majority of asthmatic patients tolerate standard fluoride varnish without any issue. The key is to have screened, to have made a reasoned clinical decision, and to have documented it.

### 4. Children Under 3

*Delivering Better Oral Health* does not routinely recommend fluoride varnish for children under 3 unless there is a clear clinical need. This is not a safety contraindication - fluoride varnish can be safely applied to very young children - but routine application is not recommended.

If a child under 3 presents with early caries or high-risk factors, fluoride varnish may be appropriate following a prescription and documented clinical rationale. This decision lies with the prescribing clinician.

### 5. Allergy to Sodium Fluoride or Other Varnish Components

Very rare, but worth noting. If a patient reports any previous reaction to fluoride products, take a detailed history and discuss with the prescribing dentist before proceeding.

## What Is NOT a Contraindication

It is equally important to know what does **not** preclude fluoride varnish application:

- **Having fillings or crowns** - varnish can be applied to any natural tooth surface
- **Using fluoride toothpaste at home** - additional topical fluoride from varnish is safe and beneficial
- **Mild anxiety** - manage with good communication techniques (tell-show-do)
- **Wearing a partial denture** - apply to natural teeth as normal
- **Pregnancy** - fluoride varnish is safe in pregnancy

## Your Pre-Treatment Safety Checklist

At each appointment, before applying fluoride varnish, document that you have checked:

1. **Asthma history** - note status and check product SDS if relevant
2. **Allergy check** - colophony/rosin, sticking plasters, sodium fluoride
3. **Soft tissue check** - no open ulceration or wounds in the area to be treated

These three checks should be recorded in every logbook entry and clinical record. If all three are clear, you can proceed with confidence. If any is flagged, make a reasoned clinical decision and document it.

---

## When to Defer and Who to Tell

If you identify a contraindication, your responsibilities are:

1. **Do not apply the varnish** to the affected area
2. **Explain to the patient** (or guardian) calmly and without alarm - for example, *"I can see there is a sore patch here, so I am going to leave that area today and we can treat it next time"*
3. **Document** the contraindication, your assessment, and the decision you made
4. **Inform the prescribing clinician** - they need to be aware, particularly if the contraindication (such as persistent ulceration) may require investigation

---

## Key Takeaway

A thorough pre-treatment assessment protects both your patient and your professional standing. The contraindications to fluoride varnish are limited and manageable - but they do require that you check actively at every visit, not just assume the situation is the same as last time. When in doubt, defer and discuss.
